TECH & HISTORY

Alltagsgeschichte.exe

How the internet took everyday life hostage — and why our tiny acts of rebellion still survive


In the 1980s, a group of German historians led by Alf Lüdtke grew tired of reading only about kings, battles, and the economy. They wanted to know what ordinary people feel and talk about in their kitchens, on their evening walks, if they ever had any. They called this approach Alltagsgeschichte – the history of everyday life.1

Central to this school is the concept of Eigen-Sinn (pronounced eye-gen-zinn): a stubborn, often silent form of self-will, not heroic rebellion, but a small act. It is the miller who reads the Bible his way, the factory worker who smokes a cigarette exactly when the foreman isn’t looking, the housewife who hides a few coins from her husband. Eigen-Sinn is the tiny act of individual rebellion against power.

Paul Veyne’s A History of Private Life showed how Romans ate, bathed, and grieved. Peter Carr’s Portavo traced a single Irish townland across centuries. These works have one thing in common: they take the mundane seriously.


I propose a small provocation: Alltagsgeschichte.exe!


Yes, the “.exe” is a nod to the executable file. We are talking about the micro-routines, anxieties, gestures, and small acts that we no longer choose.

Our everydayness is no longer our own. It is a hostage of the algorithm, the notification badge, the “seen” receipt, and the infinite scroll.

This is my Eigen-Sinn!2

We are never completely helpless! Every hostage leaves traces of stubborn agency.

In every swipe, every delayed reply, every messy folder, every private GIF, the old Eigen-Sinn survives.

Small Eigen-Sinns run in the background on the internet, which became the new ‘king, battle, and economy’. Yes, we have to call it The Internet now.

What I will do here?

Study and share about the everyday digital life of ordinary people: how we scroll, ghost, bookmark, mute, and save. More importantly, I will try to describe how people resist the machine — not with grand manifestos, but with the quiet, stubborn Eigen-Sinn.




Footnotes:


¹ The French school of the history of everyday life
The French school, often associated with the third generation of the Annales school (Philippe Ariès, Georges Duby, and Paul Veyne), focused on l’histoire de la vie privée – the history of private life. Unlike the German Alltagsgeschichte, which emphasized work, class, and Eigen-Sinn within systems of power (factories, the Nazi state, the GDR), the French tradition leaned toward mentalities (mentalités), intimacy, and the slow transformation of domestic space, bodily practices, and family structures from antiquity to modernity. While both schools rejected top-down political history, the French approach was more anthropological and less explicitly concerned with resistance to domination.

² Eigen-Sinn in classic Alltagsgeschichte
In classic Alltagsgeschichte, Eigen-Sinn is not merely “obstinacy” or “stubbornness” in a psychological sense. It describes the concrete, often unspoken way ordinary people appropriate the rules imposed upon them – bending, dodging, or reinterpreting power from below. Alf Lüdtke developed the concept while studying industrial workers and later Nazi Germany: a worker who slowed the assembly line, a housewife who listened to forbidden jazz, a soldier who wrote melancholic letters instead of heroic ones. Eigen-Sinn is the small gap between “what the system wants” and “what I actually do” – a gap where everyday humanity survives.
