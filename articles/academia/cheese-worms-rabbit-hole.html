The Cheese, the Worms, and the Rabbit Hole

What a 16th‑century miller teaches us about the internet — by negative example.


In 1583, the Inquisition burned Domenico Scandella — known as Menocchio — a miller from the Friulian village of Montereale. His crime was not heresy in the grand theological sense. It was something smaller, more intimate: he had read a handful of books (the Bible, a travelogue, a vernacular chronicle) and made up his own mind. He decided that the universe began as a lump of cheese, from which worms emerged — and those worms were the angels. Carlo Ginzburg turned this obscure trial record into The Cheese and the Worms, a masterpiece of microhistory.1

Today, we have a different kind of wandering. We call it the rabbit hole. You open a link about medieval bread. Forty‑seven minutes later, you are deep in a subreddit about conspiracy theories, then a thread about why a theory is wrong, then a YouTube video about Byzantine plumbing. You have fallen. We praise this. We call it curiosity, exploration, even resistance to the algorithm.

But we are wrong. The rabbit hole is not Menocchio’s heresy. It is almost its opposite.


Menocchio owned perhaps a dozen books. He read them obsessively, out loud, misremembering, splicing phrases from one into the other. He had no formal theology. Yet he built a cosmology that horrified the Church. Ginzburg shows us that Menocchio was not insane. He was a rational agent using the only tools he had — cheap print, memory, and the stubborn Eigen-Sinn of the powerless.

The rabbit hole, by contrast, is shallow, fast, and amnesiac. One click erases the previous. You do not build; you drift. You bookmark four hundred posts to “read later” — and never open them again. The algorithm does not punish you for wandering. It profits from your wandering. Every detour is a data point. Every “aha” moment is a click sold to an advertiser.

This is the crucial difference: Menocchio risked death by fire. The modern rabbit hole risks nothing except lost sleep and a confused sense of accomplishment. To call digital browsing “heretical” is to forget what heresy actually cost. The Church wanted conformity. The algorithm wants time on screen — it does not care if you end up believing that the moon landing was faked or that medieval peasants had wifi. Indifference is not oppression.


So what, then, is the real Eigen-Sinn — the quiet, stubborn self-will — in the age of the internet?

It is boring. It is closing the tab. Reading one article all the way through. Finishing a book. Leaving a comment thread without replying. Logging off at 10 PM. The small, unglamorous act of refusing the infinite scroll — that is the modern miller’s quiet defiance.

Consider this: Menocchio spent years with the same texts. He circled back. He reread. He argued with himself. The rabbit hole forbids circling back — there is always a new link, a fresher dopamine hit. True Eigen-Sinn in digital space means imposing your own tempo on a machine designed to accelerate you. It means reading slowly. It means saying “I have seen enough.”

The algorithm’s silent command is: keep going. Your rebellion is: stop. Not forever. Just now. That pause — that conscious, boring, unshareable pause — is the heir to the factory worker’s cigarette behind the machine, the housewife’s hidden coin, the miller’s whispered reading at midnight.


I am not romanticizing the past. Menocchio was burned alive. I am not demonizing the internet. I am typing this on a laptop, and so are you. But if we want to write an Alltagsgeschichte of the digital world — a history of everyday life inside the machine — we must stop celebrating the rabbit hole as liberation. It is not heresy. It is not resistance. It is, most of the time, just distraction with a halo.

The real question is not how deep you can fall. It is: can you still choose to climb out?




Footnotes:


¹ The Cheese and the Worms (Carlo Ginzburg, 1976)
Ginzburg’s microhistory reconstructs the worldview of Menocchio, a 16th‑century miller tried by the Inquisition. The book is a foundational text of microstoria — the Italian school of microhistory, which shares with German Alltagsgeschichte a focus on ordinary people, but places even greater emphasis on the exceptional individual as a lens for broader cultural contradictions. Menocchio’s cosmology (the cheese, the worms) was not peasant ignorance; it was a creative, syncretic bricolage of printed sources and oral tradition.

² Rabbit holes ≠ Eigen-Sinn
Alf Lüdtke’s concept of Eigen-Sinn describes how ordinary people bend imposed rules without breaking them — a worker slowing the assembly line, a soldier writing melancholic letters. The rabbit hole, however, is not a bending of rules. It is the execution of the algorithm’s deepest rule: keep attention moving. True digital Eigen-Sinn is the boring, deliberate act of stopping — reading slowly, closing tabs, logging off. It is not cinematic. It is not shareable. But it is the only everyday resistance that the machine cannot monetize.
