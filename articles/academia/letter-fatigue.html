CARMEN LÓPEZ
TECH & HISTORY

Letter fatigue

What happens to a society when the technologies of connection erode the rituals of attention?


What happens when a society built on scarcity and ceremony is flooded with more than it can process?

We are talking letters!

Any internet user now receives in a single week what an 18th-century person might not have seen in a lifetime. The result is not a golden age of connection, but a quiet epidemic of attention fatigue.

Before the post was cheap and postal networks reached into every village, a letter was not a trivial thing. It was a treasure. It arrived after days or weeks of travel, often bearing news of birth or death, marriage or ruin. You did not skim it while standing in a queue. You read it slowly, sometimes aloud, in the presence of family. You re-read it and kept it as an heirloom.

The historian Susan Whyman, in her book The Pen and the People: English Letter Writers 1660–1800, unearthed something remarkable from the archives: the "actual dialogues" of ordinary people discussing subjects as diverse as marriage, poverty, and poetry. These were not grand literary productions but the raw, intimate exchanges.

Consider the letters of the Baker family of Hertfordshire, writing letters to navigate business debts, children's marriages, and the poignant anxiety of a son seeking his fortune in London. Or the poignant petitions of impoverished women who, by mastering epistolary conventions, could petition parish officials for relief with a dignity they might never have commanded in spoken conversation. These documents were not disposable notifications!

Jeroen Blaak, in his study Literacy in Everyday Life: Reading and Writing in Early Modern Dutch Diaries, goes deeper. Analyzing four ordinary diaries, Blaak shows that reading and writing were not abstract skills but social practices embedded in the rhythms of daily life: a sailor's widow checking for news of the East India fleet, a farmer's son laboriously drafting a letter to a distant sweetheart, a craftsman noting the arrival of a printed circular in his diary alongside weather reports and debts. Reading was not a distraction from life; it was how one attended to life. It demanded patience, focus, and a willingness to sit, observe, think, and describe life.

The shift from scarcity to abundance is the great unacknowledged rupture in the history of communication and letter.

The arrival of electronic mail did not merely speed up letters; it vandalized the very form.

A letter demanded a blank page, a pen, and the commitment of time; an email demands nothing. The friction of materiality vanished, and with it, the implicit signal of care. Anyone could send anything to anyone instantly, for free, without the rituals of sealing wax or the cost of postage.

In 2025, an estimated 376.4 billion emails were sent and received every single day. By 2026, that figure is projected to rise to 392.5 billion. The average office worker now receives between 82 and 120 emails per day, translating to roughly one email every twelve minutes during waking hours. And nearly 45% of global email traffic is spam. That is almost one out of every two emails.

These numbers are so large that they are meaningless!

A 2025 longitudinal study of over a thousand schoolchildren found that digital reading habits for academic purposes in 4th grade were negatively associated with reading comprehension skills in 5th grade. Other research has confirmed that media multitasking habits are negatively associated with text comprehension. We skim; we no longer read. We scroll; we no longer attend.

It would be a mistake to imagine that spam began with the internet.

The 19th century had its own version in the form of circulars — mass-printed advertisements distributed through the post. As early as 1845, the U.S. Congress slashed postage rates for unsealed "circulars and handbills or advertisements" to encourage commercial mailings. By mid-century, a broad range of printed circulars, solicitations, and what contemporaries already called "junk mail" filled the letter bags. As one colonial-era notice for a "Lunatick Club" in 1773 explained, circular letters were simply printed invitations sent to many members at once — an 18th-century version of bulk email. The technology of mass unsolicited communication has deep roots.

But the scale of the problem today is qualitatively different. In 2025, spam accounted for 44.99% of all global email traffic, up 15% from the previous year. Malicious email attachments surged by the same percentage. The annual cost to businesses from email spam — counting lost productivity, IT protection, and security breaches — now stands at an estimated $20.5 billion annually. What was once a minor annoyance has become a chronic economic and cognitive burden, a tax on attention itself.


What happens to a society when the technologies of connection systematically erode the rituals of attention?

The answer is that we move from cherishing letters to being fatigued by them. And then, inevitably, to hating them.

Consider the unspeakable things we now do with messages that were once unthinkable with letters. We break up with a partner via SMS. We fire an employee through email. We deliver news of death via text message and Instagram posts. These acts are not merely breaches of etiquette; they are symptoms of a deeper disorder. When the medium carries no implicit weight, when the act of sending costs nothing, even the most consequential communications are flattened into the same trivial stream.

We have abundant connection but diminished relation. We are overwhelmed by messages but starved of meaning. The problem is not technological but cultural: we have forgotten how to be present to a single voice in a sea of noise. The great challenge of our era is not to send more efficiently, but to retrieve the lost art of attention. To learn again what the 18th-century letter-writers knew instinctively: that to receive a message properly is already a gift, and to respond is an act of care.

In a world of 400 billion emails a year, the most radical act may be simply to stop. To close the inbox. To read one letter all the way through. And to remember that the treasure was never the speed of the delivery, nor the number of letters, but the weight of the words.
